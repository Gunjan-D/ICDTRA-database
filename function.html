• Axon Guidance:

    Eph/ephrin signalling is crucial for the cell-cell migration of various neuronal axon types to their intended locations during the formation of the central nervous system. The ability of Eph/ephrin signalling to prevent the survival of axonal growth cones, which repels the migrating axon from the site of Eph/ephrin activation, controls the guidance of neuronal axons. Migrating axons that express either Ephs or ephrins can be directed along gradients of Eph or ephrin expressing cells towards a destination where axonal growth cone survival is likely to occur because the growth cones of migrating axons respond to relative levels of Eph and ephrin expression rather than just absolute levels.


  • Retinotopic mapping

    The proper migration of retinal ganglion cells' (RGCs') axons from the retina to particular regions in the SC, which is mediated by gradients of Eph and ephrin expression in both the SC and migrating RGCs leaving the retina, is necessary for the formation of an organised retinotopic map in the superior colliculus (SC), also known as the optic tectum in lower vertebrates. A gradient of high posterior to low anterior ephrin-A ligand expression in the SC can be used to direct migrating RGC axons from the temporal region of the retina that express a high level of EphA receptors towards targets in the anterior SC and RGCs from the nasal retina that express a low level of EphA receptors due to the decreased survival of axonal growth cones.

  • Angiogenesis

    Ephrins encourage angiogenesis in both physiological and pathological circumstances (such as neovascularization in cerebral arteriovenous malformation and cancer angiogenesis). In particular, Ephrin-B2 and EphB4 control angiogenesis by reducing expression in the VEGF signalling pathway, thereby determining the arterial and venous fate of endothelial cells, respectively. VEGF-receptors, such as VEGFR3, are affected by ephrin-B2 via both forward and reverse signalling pathways. The lymphangiogenesis stage of the Ephrin-B2 pathway results in internalisation of VEGFR3 in cultured lymphatic endothelial cells. Ephrins play a clear role in developmental angiogenesis, but their role in tumour angiogenesis is less clear. Ephrin-A2 may play a role in forward signalling during tumour angiogenesis, according to observations made in mice lacking the ephrin-A2 gene; however, this ephrin does not cause vascular malformations during development. In addition to their roles, Ephrin-B2 and EphB4 may also contribute to tumour angiogenesis.

  • Migration factor in intestinal epithelial cell migration

    Intestinal epithelial cells move steadily, precisely, and orderly from the crypt to the villus thanks to the guidance of the class A and class B ephrin protein family and the EphB family cell-surface receptors. The deeper a progenitor cell is positioned, the more the Wnt protein causes expression of the EphB receptors, which results in decreased Eph expression and increased ephrin ligand expression. The actin cytoskeleton dynamics are regulated by the engagement of the ephrin ligand with the EphB receptor, which results in a "repulsion," and this "repulsion" is what drives migration. Once the interaction comes to an end, cells don't move. Mature Paneth cells move towards the lumen as the mucus-secreting Goblet cells and the absorptive cells do.

  • Reverse signaling

    The ability of many ephrin ligands to start a "reverse" signal, which is distinct from the intracellular signal activated in Eph receptor-expressing cells, is one of their special qualities. Both ephrin-As and ephrin-Bs have been shown to mediate cellular responses that are different from those associated with activation of their corresponding receptors, despite the fact that the mechanisms by which "reverse" signalling occurs are not fully understood. Ephrin-A5 has been shown to stimulate the spreading of growth cones in spinal motor neurons, and ephrin-B1 has been shown to encourage the maturation of dendritic spines.
